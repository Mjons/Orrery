# Redshift

Light from a receding source is stretched to longer wavelengths. In
the galactic neighbourhood, that stretch is a Doppler shift from a
relative velocity; on cosmological scales, it's the expansion of space
itself stretching the photons as they travel.

[[Hubble]] noticed in 1929 that more distant galaxies have greater
redshift. That observation — the Hubble–Lemaître law — is the
empirical basis for the expanding universe.

A spectrum is the trick: the hydrogen Balmer lines and calcium H-K
lines are unmistakable, and if you see them shifted, you have a
velocity. Amateur spectroscopy can recover the redshift of 3C 273,
the brightest quasar, through an 8" scope.

Related: [[Parallax]], [[Cepheid variable]], [[M31 Andromeda]].
