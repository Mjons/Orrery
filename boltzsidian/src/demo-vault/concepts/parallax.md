# Parallax

The oldest way we measure distance to stars: watch a nearby star
shift against the distant background as the Earth moves around the
Sun. The apparent shift is half the parallax angle.

Friedrich Bessel got the first stellar parallax in 1838, for 61 Cygni.
Tiny — less than a third of an arc-second — and the Earth's orbit is
300 million kilometres across. Useful parallax measurements of
[[Sirius]] and [[Vega]] followed quickly.

Gaia, the current mission, measures parallax for a billion stars to
micro-arc-second precision. That reach ends somewhere around 50,000
light-years — after which [[Cepheid variable|Cepheids]] take over,
then [[Redshift]] for anything further.

The cosmic distance ladder starts here.
